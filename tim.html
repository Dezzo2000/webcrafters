Tim Berners-Lee is a British computer scientist known for inventing the World Wide Web in 1989. His creation revolutionized the way people access and share information, shaping the modern internet. Berners-Lee’s vision was to create a universal platform for knowledge, and his work laid the foundation for the global connectivity we experience today. Tim Berners-Lee was born on June 8, 1955, in London, into a family of mathematicians and engineers. His parents were both professionals in the field of computer science, and this family environment contributed to his early curiosity about technology and mathematics. He graduated in Physics from the University of Oxford in 1976, where he stood out for his work on circuits and computers. After graduation, in 1980, he moved to CERN (the European Organization for Nuclear Research) in Geneva, where his destiny changed

In 1989, Tim Berners-Lee came up with his pioneering idea for a system that would allow documents to be linked and navigated through hyperlinks. The idea was not just to create a system for sharing information at CERN, but to make it possible to connect content from all over the world, so that people could access resources and information in a more immediate and effective way. In 1990, Berners-Lee began working concretely on the project and in 1991, the first website in history was created and launched online. This site, hosted on a computer at CERN, explained what the World Wide Web was and how to use it. Not only that, but Berners-Lee made a historic decision: he chose to make the web code open-source and free. This allowed anyone to use, modify, and further develop it, a step that had a profound impact on the rapid spread of the technology and the creation of a global network.

On August 6, 1991, he opened his website (info.cern.ch) to public access over the Internet. He posted instructions for how to set up web servers and create sites, providing all the software he had created, free of charge. He announced his creation through a few Internet mail groups. In 1994, to oversee the growth of the Web and to promote the adoption of common standards, Berners-Lee founded the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C) at MIT (Massachusetts Institute of Technology). The W3C is an international organization that develops and promotes web standards to ensure that the web remains open, accessible, and compatible across different platforms. Tim Berners-Lee, the inventor of the World Wide Web, noticed over time that the web was becoming increasingly controlled by large tech companies that centralized personal data. This raised concerns about privacy and the open nature of the web. To address these issues, in 2018, Berners-Lee launched Solid, a project aimed at creating a decentralized web and to restore a sense of digital democracy, give all users the ability to restrict access to their own information and to whatever content they create on the web. In autumn 2018, Berners-Lee announced the launch of the first major commercial venture to be built on the Solid platform: Inrupt, a browser which combines the functions of popular Internet apps such as Google Drive, Microsoft Outlook, Slack, Spotify, and WhatsApp, allowing users to access all of their data in a single customized personal interface. Inrupt users will store their information in PODs (personalized online data stores), free of contact with the largest Internet companies, which have been widely criticized for selling user information to third parties.
